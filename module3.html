Reflective Piece

The Secure Software Development module is more intensive to allow students to apply the fundamental principles we learned in previous modules. We are supposed to examine security risks associated with programming languages from the perspectives of design and architecture approaches, programming paradigms, testing, the impact and implications of operating systems and libraries on software development, the security risks associated with distributed systems and APIs, and future trends in secure software and systems design.

We learned about secure development methodologies and best practices for designing safe and compliant software over the course of twelve weeks.

UML remains the most effective tool in describing different components and their interaction within a system in this module. I had a chance to review my knowledge of UML diagrams by illustrating the steps to exploit one of the weaknesses identified by OWASP. We also teamed up this week to prepare for the group assignment.

The first team activity was to analyze how secured Python is and determine what factors define the safety of a programming language. Along with that, I was exploring the use of regular expressions and recursion in creating software solutions to better understand the concepts and the pros and cons of both approaches. It was a surprise to know that we could get the maximum recursion depth in Python and set it to the desired number. There is no pre-set minimum recursion depth in most languages, causing overflow of the stack and crash the program. Learning how to avoid ReDOS was another great experience while exploring the usage of regular expressions (Regex)

The next team discussion was to explore the cyclomatic complexity and a few other testing tools available in Python. The coding activity covered a wide range of test cases where I can compare different testing tools and linters in terms of their efficiency in enforcing the coding format and analyzing the inconsistency in the coding convention.

Our team was supposed to deliver the design document of a secure repository by the end of unit 6. The goal was to focus on the security aspects and apply as many proper tools and techniques as possible to secure our application. In addition, we got to think about the system's scalability and how it would comply with the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) . I struggled at first, learning how to work with teammates from different professions and backgrounds. Time management and communication are soft skills that I have improved during this time. Because of the time-zone difference, I can rarely attend live seminars. Thankfully, one of my teammates was supportive in keeping our team and the tutor engaged so we could constantly get feedback for our work.

In the next couple of units, we kept diving into other security aspects by doing some coding activities and studying cryptography in connection with the operating systems to figure out why it is crucial to encrypt the data. A case study was brought up in our forum discussion regarding TrueCrypt's discontinuation of development. Besides the exciting story of its creator's anonymity, it seems to me that some fundamental software engineering principles are missing in the TrueCrypt project. Most of the vulnerabilities pointed out by the security audit evaluation of the software itself illustrate a lack of software development best practices and proper quality control processes.

Continuing to expand my knowledge on the capabilities of different Python libraries, I went through a section to develop a restful API for a distributed environment to prepare for our final submission in unit 11 â€“ the coding output. I would consider this was a heavy module due to our lack of knowledge of available Python libraries. We chose to use Python and the Flask framework to inherit the knowledge from the previous modules. The knowledge inheritance helped us build out a foundational structure for our system; the challenging part was trying out, selecting, and plugging the right tools into our application. Not all the features we designed have been fully implemented, but our team has given effort and attention to incorporating as many aspects of learning from this module to the submission.

I'm leaving the module realizing that security is now a requirement for software development and should not be taken lightly. Most vulnerabilities are found in the application's source code rather than in other layers. Fortunately, most common software security flaws can be mitigated by adhering to well-established secure coding standards.
